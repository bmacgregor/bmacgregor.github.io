Damage Types

Melee and missile weapons deal one of three wound types: Cleaving, Piercing, or Blunt wounds.

Cleaving wounds are the most common form of wound delivered in a fight.

Piercing wounds caused by spear and sword thrusts, incoming arrows, and the like, do their work internally, and are in many ways deadlier that sword cuts. They normally use the thrust Target Zones (11-14).

Blunt wounds are caused by maces, clubs, quarterstaffs, pommels and the like, including bare fists, can be deadly. The impact of such strikes can cause large amounts of Shock (see below), which often necessitates difficult Knockdown Checks.

Damage Types Described

Bleeding from Piercing wounds is usually much harder to control as such wounds are typically located deep within the victim’s body.

Blunt attacks are effective against many types of armour, as much of a blow’s force will transmit through armour without the need to penetrate, causing the wearer substantial blunt trauma. Blunt attacks are usually swung (areas 1 – 7), but some, including those delivered from fists, may be executed with a straight thrust (areas 8 - 14).

For all other forms of damage there is a Generic Damage Table, which can be found in the Appendix.

Shock and Pain are both metrics indicating how badly any wound affects a combatant’s ability to persevere and keep fighting. Each wound descriptor contains an individual rating for both.

Shock

Shock is only applied once for each wound, but deducts dice from all three dice pools (Melee, Archery, and Sorcery) the moment the character is struck by the blow. If the received Shock exceeds a Character’s total current Melee Pool for his very next action, the remaining of the Shock penalty is instead added to the aggressor’s Melee Pool for the next Combat Round or Exchange. Such bonus dice are only usable against the Shocked opponent.

Pain

Pain is deducted from each of the three pools at the beginning of each Combat Round, starting with the one following the Combat Round in which Shock from the wound was applied. While Shock is a fixed number, Pain is reduced by one’s Tenacity. For the purposes of wound healing, where Pain is important, record the unreduced Pain rating of every wound, but apply only Pain in excess of the character’s Tenacity as a penalty to her pools.

Tenacity is applied in full to Pain with every single wound; do not total up Pain from several wounds first and then apply the Tenacity score. Instead, apply Tenacity to every single wound and then add up the reduced Pain ratings to determine the penalty to die pools.

In addition to the die penalty to one’s applicable pool from Blood Loss (BL), described next, a Pain component is also added to a roll when tasked with a Skill or Attribute Check for any activity requiring fast, controlled movement, such as riding at a gallop, running, climbing, jumping, or other similar tasks.

Such residual Pain is mechanically expressed as one additional Success needed for every four dice removed by Pain. If the roll does not yield the required additional net Successes, the attempted Skill or Attribute Check has failed. Of course, if the Character is suffering from less than four cumulative points of Pain, no additional Successes are required, and the normal amount of Successes will suffice for Checks.

Example: Otho is at Pain four while plodding along the King’s Highway on horseback. Brigands suddenly burst onto the roadway behind him in hot pursuit; Otho kicks his mount into a gallop! The Game Master asks Otho’s Player to make a Riding Skill Check, needing at least one additional Success in order to cope with his Pain and stay in the saddle. He rolls two Successes, and is able to keep his seat when his horse explodes into a gallop, gritting his teeth against the pain.


Pain is also important in figuring out healing times, as described in Chapter Five.

Shock and Pain are cumulative as long as they are applied to different zones. Should one attack the same area multiple times, the highest shock penalty — new or old — is applied (or re-applied) to the wounded party. Because of this, it can be beneficial to attack a wounded area again and again, even with weak attacks — the original shock penalty will keep on returning to haunt the wounded!

When recording wounds on your Character sheet, make a note of where the exact wound is and what effects it may have, such as Shock, Pain, or Blood Loss. Such shorthand as “Wound Level 3, cut, 6/4 left,” meaning “A level three Cleaving wound at zone 6, area 4 (from the d6 roll), on the left side". Make sure to record each individual wound’s Pain, Shock, and Blood Loss ratings as well.

Blood Loss

Blood Loss covers the deterioration of one’s body due to bleeding, the most dangerous of which is the internal kind. Some wounds, especially of the Piercing and Cleaving type, carry a Blood Loss value along with Shock and Pain ratings.

The Blood Loss ratings of all wounds sustained are cumulative provided they come from different parts of the body. Each limb, the entire torso, and the head each count as induvidual locations. If a body part already sports a wound with an attendant Blood Loss rating and is wounded again in a way to indicate further Blood Loss, only the higher of the two ratings comes (or stays) in effect. At the very end of the Limelight in which the Character first acquired Blood Loss, and at the end of each subsequent Limelight, she must check Brawn. This Check must yield Successes equal to or surpassing the current Blood Loss number. If it doesn’t, the Character suffers a two die penalty to her Melee, Archery, and Spell Pools. In addition, her Attribute and Skill Checks will need an additional Success per full four dice penalty. The Brawn Check to avoid further Blood Loss is exempt from this requirement for additional Successes. These penalties represent dizziness or weakness from loss of blood, and accumulate from failed Brawn Check to failed Brawn Check.

Once accumulated penalties from failed Blood Loss Checks equal or exceed the character’s Brawn, the Character passes out from blood loss, and will likely die without quick medical attention. Thereafter, a Blood Loss Check is made for the unconscious Character at the end of each subsequent Round of Limelights. If any Check fails, the Character has bled to death. Blood Loss Checks made while the bleeding Character is at rest (no more strenuous activity than walking very slowly) require one less Success than indicated by the current Blood Loss rating to be successful. This includes Characters’ unconscious from Blood Loss.

Blood Loss ratings, unless from internal damage, are easily removed by the most elemental use of first aid (for details on healing, see Chapter Five), however, even if a Character’s Blood Loss rating is reduced to zero, those penalties accrued due to Blood Loss will not diminish immediately. Such penalties are reduced by two for each die Pool for each passing day spent resting with proper nourishment and low activity, or by one per passing day of exertion.

Knockdown

Knockdown can be the result of some wounds, especially (but not exclusively) those to the legs. With such wounds, the description will include a Knockdown number which denotes the number of Successes needed on a Knockdown Check to avoid falling prone. A Knockdown Check is also necessitated each time a Character’s remaining Melee Pool for the Round is reduced to zero or less from a wound received. The Challenge Rating of this Check will be set at Challenging (two successes) if the Character’s Melee Pool is reduced to below –5, or simply Average (one success) in other cases. If the wound which caused the initial Shock reduced the Melee Pool to zero or below and also calls for a Check, only the more difficult of the two Checks need be made, but the Challenge Rating of the Check is increased by 1.

Optionally, if a group wants an additional layer of realism, every hit to the knee or below that fails to cause a wound instead generates a Knockdown Check. The Challenge Level of this Check is Average (one success) if the hit was from a thrust-based attack or one-handed swing, Challenging (two successes) if from a two-handed swing or a one-handed swing with a mass weapon, and Difficult (three successes) if from a two-handed swing with a mass weapon or pole-arm. However, the required number of Successes may never be greater than the Quality of Success on the attack roll; light blows to the legs won’t stagger many opponents, even if delivered by massive weapons.

As another option, any hit at the elbow or below to the weapon arm of somebody wielding a weapon in one hand can also necessitate a Knockdown of the same Challenge Level as above, failure resulting in the weapon being dropped.

A prone Character can use only half of their Melee Pool while fighting from the ground. The number of dice used is halved after all other modifiers (Shock, Pain, Blood Loss, etc.) have been applied. If he is knocked down during an ongoing Combat Round, he immediately loses a number of dice from his Melee Pool equal to the difference between his Melee Pool at the beginning of the Round and half that number.

Example: Togrul begins a Round with MP 13. If he is knocked down during this Round, he immediately loses 6 dice from his Melee Pool (the difference between 13 and 7). Provided nothing else deducts from his Melee Pool, he refreshes his Pool to 7 at the beginning of the next Round.

To get back on his feet while fighting an opponent, he must win a defense Check in a way that would otherwise enable him to assume the role of aggressor. In this case, he can choose between either remaining the defender but getting back to his feet, or becoming the aggressor but remaining on the ground.


Knockout

Knockout can be a consequence of blows to the head. If the description of a wound contains a Knockout number, this is the Challenge Level the wounded Character needs to pass on an immediate Check of her Knockout Attribute. Failure on this check indicates unconsciousness. Optionally, any blow to the head that fails to cause a wound will still call for a Knockout Check nonetheless, unless the hit was received from a thrust Piercing or thrust Cleaving attack.

The Challenge Level of this Check is Average (one success) if a swung Piercing attack, Challenging (two successes) if a swung Cleaving or thrusting Blunt attack, and Difficult (three successes) if a swung Blunt attack, but may be under no circumstances higher than half the attack’s Quality of Success.
