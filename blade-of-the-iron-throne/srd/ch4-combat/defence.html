Defence

Defence focuses squarely on a warrior’s efforts to avoid being struck by an opponent.

Evasion

Evasion can be done by anyone and used with every Proficiency. It doesn’t carry any Activation Cost, unless the Character is encumbered and the optional Encumbrance rules are being used. In that case, there is an Activation Cost; it can be found on the Encumbrance Terrain Modifier table.

The table below provides Target Numbers for the three common forms of evasion:

Evasion Target Numbers

Five Full Evasion
Eight Partial Evasion
Ten Duck and Weave

Full Evasion

Full Evasion - if successful - brings all melee to a halt as the fighters separate and possibly attempt to re-establish initiative. The bout ends for the moment, and stances can once again be assumed. If a Character conducts a Full Evasion during an Exchange, the Quality of Success is added as bonus dice.

Partial Evasion

Partial Evasion - if successful - does not allow the defender to assume the role of aggressor as usual. No matter what the actual Quality of Success of the successful Partial Evasion, assuming the role of aggressor requires the immediate expenditure of 2 Melee Pool points. If the successful defender is unable (or unwilling) to spend those dice, the aggressor retains his role.

Duck & Weave

Duck and Weave - if successful - gives the evading warrior a great opportunity to strike her opponent. She automatically assumes the role of aggressor (even if her Quality of Success was merely zero) and moves into the optimum range for her weapon’s Reach. Her opponent may not employ a shield against her follow-up attack on the next Exchange, and loses dice from her Melee Pool equal to the Successes on the attack roll Duck and Weave defended against. Should this penalty take the Melee Pool into the negative range, those dice are added as bonus dice to the other party’s Melee Pool.

Evading Missile Attacks

Evasion can also be used to defend against missile attacks, provided the defender is aware of them. The Target Number for this is 8 (Partial Evasion) for thrown weapons, and 10 (Duck & Weave) for arrows, crossbow bolts, and bullets.

If the defender is aware that a gun, bow, or crossbow is being aimed at him, and has the opportunity to start evasive movement before the missile is fired, the Evasion Target Number is now merely 8 (Partial Evasion). This represents weaving around in a highly unpredictable way. If the Character is engaged in a bout of melee combat, his unpredictable combat moves automatically count as this type of weaving movement, allowing him to use the Partial Evasion Target Number against a missile attack.

Evading missile attacks as outlined above is also possible when a Character is not in a melee situation. In this case, she may use dice equal to half her best Melee Pool rating, even if she may not currently have the equipment necessary for fighting with that Proficiency at hand. Applicable Passion Attributes figure normally in this halved Melee Pool rating (they are added before dividing the pool in two).

When a Character uses either Partial Evasion or Duck & Weave as a defence against a melee or a missile attack, the result of this single defence roll is applied against all missile attacks aimed at him during this Exchange. Equally, if a Character not currently in a melee is fired upon and uses Partial Evasion to defend against a missile attack, the result of this single roll is applied against all missile attacks on him within this Combat Round. Should he be targeted by both thrown and launched weapons which require different Target Numbers to defend against (8 and 10), a single roll is still used to defend against all of the attacks, but this roll will still cover the two different Quality of Successes required; one against the Target Number of 8 and one against the Target Number of 10.

Example: Otho evades both a javelin and a crossbow. He is not aware of the crossbow, so his Defence Target Number against the javelin is 8 and against the crossbow bolt 10... His Melee Pool is 13, but as he isn't in a melee, he rolls half his of his Melee Pool (7 dice). The dice come up 2, 5, 6, 7, 8, 10, 12 – he has 3 Successes against the javelin (8, 10, 12) and 2 against the crossbow (10, 12).

If somebody fires into a melee combat, the missile attack is always resolved on Exchange 1.

Defensive Actions

The bedrock of all Defensive Maneuvers discussed is the Block, the Parry, and Evasion. Most defensive Maneuvers are directly tied to these basics. Parrying is the preferred defence of warriors everywhere. Most warriors grasp the idea of turning the attack of an opponent’s weapon with their own. Any amount of Melee Pool dice may be devoted to parrying while defending. The challenge of deflecting another’s weapon is reflected by the defending weapon’s Defence Target Number (DTN).

Some weapon schools, for instance those of the Cut & Thrust Proficiency, teach using a secondary parrying weapon, usually a dagger, in the off-hand, allowing the person to potentially parry twice in a single Exchange, or to perform Maneuvers like Simultaneous Block/Strike. Although parrying does not involve great manual dexterity, a person’s secondary arm is still handicapped. The Defence Target Number of any parry conducted with the off-hand is therefore increased by one.

Blocking

Blocking is the action of interposing an object, usually a shield, between oneself and the attack, perhaps in a rather unsophisticated way, but also by skilfully angling a shield or using it offensively to beat aside an attacking weapon. In this use, it is similar to the parry. Every blocking object is assigned a Target Number for defending with it, this Target Number being dependent on the size of the object, and how quickly and gracefully it can be moved. It is thus almost identical to a weapon’s Defence Target Number, but with one important difference: Shields (and only shields, not improvised blocking objects like chairs and the like) are designed to be used in the off-hand, and therefore suffer no penalty for being used with the wrong hand.

Shields

Shields are divided into three types, depending on size: large, small, and bucklers. A large shield is just that – a very large shield, measuring over one meter in length if an oblong shape, or having a diameter of not much less than one meter if more squarish or round. It offers superior protection but is unwieldy to carry about and very awkward to use on horseback as one’s horse constantly gets in the way of the shield. On the positive side, most large shields are big enough to completely hunker down behind, removing its owner, provided he remains static, from the danger of missile attacks.

A small shield is anything larger than a buckler and smaller than a large shield. It is versatile, and of equal use on foot or horseback.

Finally, a buckler is a small, agile shield. It isn't strapped to the arm, but instead, carried in a fist-grip. It's rarely any larger than 0.3 meters in diameter. All types of shields have variable Defence Target Numbers against different forms of attack such as melee, missile attacks with thrown weapons such as javelins or hatchets, or against fired projectiles like arrows, crossbow bolts, and sling stones – though fired objects are often so fast (causing the defender to roll vs. a high Defence Target Number) as to often rule out blocking as a viable defence against fired missiles.

The applicable Defensive Target Number depends on both the type of shield, and the attack being resisted. See the table, below:

Shield Defence Target Numbers

Shield Type Versus Melee Versus Thrown Versus Projectile
Large 5 6 7
Small 6 7 9
Buckler 7 9 11

Armour Overview

The function of armour is to reduce or prevent injury to someone being hit in armoured spot. It does so by reducing the Wound Level of any hit to an armoured location by the Armour Value (AV) of that armour. If the Wound Level is reduced to zero or less, no wound is inflicted. Armour in Blade doesn't apply to all locations of the armoured person, but to each location only to the extent that armour is worn in the spot being struck.

Someone wearing a short tunic of sturdy leather and an iron helmet will have a different Armour Value to the crown of her head than to her guts or shins. Because of this, it is essential to record which spot is armoured, and to what degree. The Blade Character sheet provides space for such notation. Armour doesn’t provide the same Armour Value against all types of damage; a thickly padded gambeson provides decent protection against Blunt damage, but much less so against Piercing. Each armour type has three Armour Values; one against Piercing, one against Blunt, and one against Cleaving. These Armour Values can be found in the table below.

Not all armours have an Armour Value against Cleaving damage. Metal armours are virtually impossible to cut through with any weapon driven by human strength; the most that can be achieved is a dent or a small rip, a far cry from a serious wound. This doesn’t mean that the wearer of such armour is immune against Cleaving weapons – even if a blade does not actually cut through the armour, the impact will still transfer Blunt trauma, possibly breaking bones, or perhaps even killing the wearer, all without seriously damaging the armour.

For this reason, all weapon stats in the Appendix include Blunt Damage Ratings. Armour lacking a Cleaving Value uses the Blunt Armour Value against Cleaving attacks, while attacking weapons calculate damage inflicted against such armour use their Blunt Damage Ratings.

Protection Type Versus Piercing Versus Blunt Versus Cleaving
Gambeson 1 2 2
Leather 2 1 2
Leather with Metal 2 1 3
Hardened Leather 3 2 2
Leather / Horn Scale / Lamellar * 3 3 3
Metal Scale / Lamellar * 4 4 n/a
Brigandine * 4 4 n/a
Maille * 4 3 n/a

* Requires the wearing of a gambeson to be functional. If such armour is worn without a gambeson, all Armour Values are halved.

** Use the lesser value if suits of articulated plate are not available, otherwise, use the greater value for the head and torso and the lesser value for the limbs. Requires the use of a gambeson.

3. Armour Types

Gambeson

Pliable textile armour made of a number of layers of quilted cloth and stuffed with cotton, animal hair or other padding. Many other types of armour require the wearing of a gambeson underneath to function properly, which is why the gambeson is also called an arming doublet.

Leather

Tough, yet pliable, cured hide.

Leather with Metal

Pliable leather with short metal strips or rings sewn in strategic places to guard against cuts.

Hardened Leather

Cured hide boiled and then dried to make it harder at the cost of most of its pliability. It can be fashioned into segments large enough to serve as helmets and greaves, but not for workable breastplates.

Leather / Horn Scale / Lamellar

Armour fashioned from strips of either hardened leather or animal bone. For scale armour, the strips are sewn onto a backing of soft leather or heavy canvas, and then overlapped like roofing tiles. For lamellar armour, each strip has holes fashioned into its edges and are then bound together and overlapped by two neighbouring pieces while yet again overlapping others. Lamellar is therefore quite rigid, while scale is minimally pliable. Still, neither can be used to protect a joint, and both require the wearing of a gambeson underneath.

Brigandine

Armour made of palm- to hand-sized metal plates, each sandwiched between two layers of heavy canvas or soft leather and riveted together to stay in place, also known as Coat of Plates. Brigandine is quite rigid and cannot be used to protect joints. It requires wearing of a gambeson underneath. Brigandine Armour Values can also be used for Imperial Roman legionaries’ segmented armour.

Maille

Also known as chainmail, this armour consists of a mesh of small metal rings, each interlinked with neighbouring rings. It is supremely pliable and can therefore be used to protect joints, but it, too, is dependent on a gambeson being worn underneath.

Brigandine over Maille

A special blend of layering armours, this was common during the transitional period from maille to plate, and consists of maille with additional brigandine worn over it to protect the torso from shoulder to hip.

Plate

Metal plates shaped to encase the body, including helmets. Plate armour requires the wearing of a gambeson, which along with an articulated suit of plate may also have insets of maille.
