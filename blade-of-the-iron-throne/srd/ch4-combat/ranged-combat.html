Ranged Combat Overview

In Blade, ranged combat can include attacks from a thrown rock to a steel-pronged crossbow and everything in between. Attacks may sometimes occur as a single, sniper-like shot with no chance for the target to react, but more frequently the attack will be launched into a melee, or will perhaps lead to a melee situation. Such attacks are rolled with dice from the Character’s Archery Pool, and consists of (Aim + Missile Proficiency).

Ranged Combat (Continued)

For the latter case, ranged combat must be integrated with melee combat in order to give archers a place in the Order of Limelights. This spot in the Limelight sequence is determined exactly as if the archer was a melee combatant, with the same principles holding true for both participants.

That is to say that sometimes, when it is clear who is going first (for example when an encounter is initiated by a crossbow bolt from an ambush), the Game Master will establish the Order of Limelights by calling Players in a logical order. However, in cases where the sequence is more random, Players will be called to play out their Characters’ Limelights in ascending order of Cunning, as described in the section on Limelights and the Combat Round.

Making a Ranged Attack

During his Limelight, a Character intending to launch a missile attack can do so, provided he has the weapon at hand. There is no preparation, loading, or aiming times, as a Limelight is considered a span of time sufficient to accomplish a significant task, which includes firing a weapon, not merely preparing to fire it. As melee combatants move about during their own Limelights, distances and line of sight usually won't be apparent during the ranged combatant's Limelight. It's up to the Game Master to decide these things based upon common sense, the Players’ description of their Characters’ actions, and the scene’s dramatic requirements.

If the Player of a missile combatant is not happy with the GM's judgement, she can have her character move into a more advantageous firing position. Such movement to get an unobstructed line of sight - or merely closer to the target - is accomplished with a Terrain Check made with dice from the character’s Archery Pool. The Game Master announces the Challenge Level to get to closer range or to achieve a free line of sight (rarely harder than Difficult - two successes - if even that). The Player then attempts to pass the Check using dice out of her Archery Pool, rolling any amount of dice desired.

Success means that the Character achieves her objective, while failure signifies that she must either take the shot at a longer range, or has to contend with cover obscuring the target, and occasionally, she might have to deal with both circumstances (at a longer range and shooting past cover) – it never means that a shot is outright impossible. A Terrain Check can also be used when a missile combatant is charged by an enemy and must shoot her before being engaged in melee. In such cases, the Game Master decides the Challenge Level based on how close the charging party currently is, and how ready the missile combatant is to fire.

Casting a spear that is ready in hand before being engaged shouldn't be harder than Average (one success), while spanning and discharging a heavy crossbow may be as difficult as Legendary (five successes), provided the attacker isn’t very far off. If both opponents are important Characters or NPCs, the Terrain Check is an Opposed one. The charging combatant declares how many dice out of his Melee Pool he is going to spend to engage before he can be fired at, and then the missile combatant declares the number of dice from his Archery Pool he is going to spend. Depending on the Game Master's appraisal of the situation (distance of the opponent versus the readiness of the missile weapon), one opponent will likely need a Quality of Success of just one to succeed, whereas the other may well need a higher Quality of Success.

Whether the situation is handled by an Opposed Check or not, victory on the part of the missile combatant will mean that he gets a shot off at practically point blank range. Failure, however, signifies that he will be engaged before he can fire, and that the Combat Round immediately commences with the missile combatant’s initial Melee Pool reduced by the number of dice the ranged Character spent from his Archery Pool on the Terrain Check.

If he was trying to throw a spear or similar weapon useful in close combat, he may opt to retain it in his hand, combat-ready. Otherwise, he is assumed to have realized that his attempt to shoot his opponent would not pan out, has dropped his missile weapon, and barely managed to whip out a melee weapon – if he has one.

Range Increments

Range, like many aspects of Blade combat, is handled in an abstract way. First and foremost, it depends on the principle that the ultimate limitation to all range is human vision - it doesn’t matter that a heavy crossbow can launch a bolt over 300 yards if one can’t discern the target at such a distant range. Even missile weapons with theoretical maximum ranges as long as some bows and crossbows have realistic practical ranges that are only a fraction of that maximum. Extreme ranges are for wide-shooting contests and battlefield area-fire, not for taking down individual targets.

Practical ranges vary from two to one-hundred meters, and are divided into Range Bands from Point Blank to Extreme. The actual distance each Range Band covers is different for each type of ranged weapon. Weapons that are thrown in such a way that they tumble while in the air (knives and hatchets) have the least range, while those that are cast to fly straight (spears and javelins) can achieve a middling range, and finally those that are launched (arrows, bolts and sling stones) travel the furthest.

Each Range Band determines how easy or difficult it is to aim for a specific body part by governing how many dice from the Archery Pool must be spent for a single +/- 1 modifier on the following Table. The Target Zone of a ranged attack is determined randomly by rolling 2d6 on the Ranged Attack Zones table. That said, a missile combatant has some degree of control over the exact Target Zone, and can spend dice from her Archery Pool before rolling the attack.

The number of dice needed to be spent for a single +/- 1 move on the Ranged Attack Zones table is listed in each range band found in the following table (Range Band Modifiers). Also note that at Point Blank range, the missile combatant receives a free modifier of +/- 2; if your target is right in front of you, you're not likely to aim either high or low. Apart from this free +/- 2, the cost for further modifiers is the same as Near range. This modifier does not have to be spent in full on the Ranged Attack Zones table; it can also be spent in part or not at all, depending on the Player’s wishes.

Cover

Cover can complicate a ranged attack. While some obstacles can be avoided by means of a Terrain Check, this isn't possible in all cases – no maneuvering in the world will do away with the cover given by a breastwork to defenders manning a wall. Soft cover that obstructs vision but doesn't completely block it, like foliage or a moving crowd of people, imparts a negative modifier to the Archery Pool.

Depending on the GM’s judgement of how obscured the target is by soft cover, this modifier can range from –1 to –3 per line on the Range Band Modifier table. The possible modifiers are therefore for example –1, –2, or –3 at Point Blank range (line one on the Range Band Modifier table), and –3, –6, and –9 at Medium range. It can range from a mild –1 for a target at Point Blank range past a single person moving through the line of sight, to a whopping –15 for a target at Extreme range that is made almost invisible by the densest of foliage.

The rules for soft cover also apply if one wants to fire into an on-going melee and hit one combatant while avoiding the other, a difficult feat due to the unpredictable movements of both. In this case, the minimum penalty for soft cover (ex. –1 per line on the Range Band Modifier table, effectively –1 to –5) is used – and a Botch on the attack roll usually means a hit on a person the missile combatant doesn't want to hit.

If that happens, the attack roll is repeated. The original penalty dice for firing into a melee still apply for this roll, but neither penalty dice for soft cover or any dice possibly set aside for buying modifiers on the Ranged Attack Zones table do. Any such penalty dice on the original roll are added to the new attack roll, and any hit resulting from this second roll applied to the new target. The exact attack location on the new target is determined randomly on the Ranged Attack Zones table with no modifiers to this roll.

Example: Otho is throwing his dagger at Far range to aid his friend Galen in his melee against two opponents. Otho has an Archery Pool of 13. Four dice are subtracted for firing into a melee at Far range, and a further three dice for buying a +/– 1 modifier on the Ranged Attack Zones table. Otho Botches the attack roll with his remaining six dice, and has to reroll his attack, now with Galen as target. The four dice penalty for firing into a melee still applies, but the three dice traded in for a +/– 1 modifier don’t; his Archery Pool is therefore 10.

Should the attack hit, the damage location will be determined randomly on the Ranged Attack Zones table, without the +/– 1 modifier Otho had purchased for his original attack.

Hard cover that cannot be fired through represents a more extreme problem for a missile combatant. If it can’t be circumvented to get a clear shot, the attacker will just have to take his chances by investing heavily into modifiers on the Ranged Attack Zones table and hope that this modifier is sufficient to hit a location which isn't covered.

Defending against missile attacks is only possible against attacks that one is aware of. It is preferably done with a large shield, or alternatively by evading. Details on that process and its associated Target Numbers can be found in the section on defending.
