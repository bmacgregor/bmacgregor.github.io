Creating Your Own Talent Table

The Talent table as presented in this chapter is just one of many possible incarnations of this table; one that will probably suit most games of Blade, but not necessarily all.

To some settings, such as gritty historical ones, the Culture column might not apply; others might be either devoid of anything supernatural or keeps such elements strictly out of players’ hands, and thus have no Sorcery column; and while the Asset column will likely be common, not all referees will be interested in adding it.

Creating Your Own Talent Table (Continued)

The only three columns that are universal to all games of Blade are Attributes, Proficiencies, and Skills. Some, but by no means all, possible ideas for the other priorities are:


Race

In campaigns where non-human races are more common than the default for Blade, the group might want to allow players access to such races as Characters. A Race priority would regulate access to the various racial packages.

Social

In some campaigns the Players might have the option to have their Characters belong to widely different social classes. A Social priority would give access to anything from slave to nobleman, with the attendant wealth and legal privileges or limitations.

Connections

Especially in campaigns of the cloak-and-dagger type, the extent of a Character's access to local flows of power and information may be relevant. A Connections priority would determine how well-connected a Character is.

Status

In some campaigns all Characters might be members of the same organization, for instance a cult or an army. A Status priority in such campaigns could be used to determine a Character's status within the organization, from grunt to mover and shaker.

Design Notes

The design of Blade endorses and encourages this type of column customization; take them, make them your own, and remold them to best support the game you want to play. There’s no need to have a column that isn’t truly important in your game. To help you design your own columns, here are the basics of how we have designed ours:

The D Pick is considered to be average, providing no special benefit or disadvantage. Descending the line from D, E is noticeably worse, though not devastatingly so; the difference amounts to two Attribute or Proficiency points, six Skill points, or one Asset.

The F Pick is intended to be truly poor and is set about three times as far below in value to the D Pick as the E Pick is; the equivalent of about six Attribute or Proficiency points, eighteen Skill points, or three Assets seems about right to us.

Our choice of numbering here is very deliberate; we wanted every step on the table away from D to be larger than the one preceding it. This uneven stepping also applies when one ascends the line of the table from the average D Pick.

The C Pick equally scales improvement from the D Pick as much the E Pick worsens it; C and E are intended to mirror the other’s separation from the D Pick.

In contrast, the B Pick is not intended to mirror the F Pick in the same way. Instead there is a less drastic improvement from C compared to the F Pick’s drop from D; the equivalent of three Attribute or Proficiency points, nine Skill points, or two Assets better than C seems to us to be on target.

The A Pick is obviously intended to be the most advantageous, and thus is about twice as rewarding compared to the D Pick than the B Pick; the difference from A to D amounting to about ten Attribute or Proficiency points, thirty Skill points, or six Assets.

So now you have our architecture of the Talent table; now go forth and customize your own!
