Characters and Talents

Characters in Blade are painted in broad strokes by their Talents. These Talents, primarily Attributes, Skills, and Proficiencies, are used by the player as a metric of their Character’s strengths and weaknesses.

Players can and should consult the wide body of Sword & Sorcery literature available for their inspiration. Joining in with their Game Maser and fellow players, they can then exchange ideas and work on creating Character histories (and Passions) that will work in harmony with the rest of the group’s goals and story direction.

Characters and Talents (Continued)

This is where the Talent Table now comes into play. Our particular Blade Talent Table is comprised of six Talents. While the described categories of Attributes, Skills, nd Proficiencies form the heart of the entries on the Blade Talent Table, there are many more possibilities that could be made for your game.

Talents

Having by now gotten a sense of the Character that they wish to build stories around, Players then translate these ideas to Blade by selecting Talent levels based on their importance to the player’s vision of their Character. Will the character be a man of fierce fighting ability? Or, perhaps a deft thief prowling the rooftops of fantastic cities! The prime focus of the Character should be their A or B picks.

Next come aspects of the character that should still demonstrate competence or training. These will likely merit a C or D pick.

Last comes those picks that the player has determined are not of major concern to his Blade experience, the E and F picks. Bear in mind, these last choices are where the Character will be at their weakest - choose carefully!

Sorcery

The lands of the Sword & Sorcery campaign, though dominated by skillful swordsmen, are haunted by powerful Sorcerers wielding their dark magic. The Sorcery Priority governs the relation of your Character to the occult mysteries of the supernatural, whether your character is steeped in them, a regular person, or victimized by those dark forces. If you plan on playing a non-sorcerer Character, then select either Mundane as your Priority D (the Average priority), or Cursed as your Priority E. Selecting Doomed as your Priority F also gives you a character without sorcerous powers, but shouldn’t be done lightly; the character is not likely to survive this choice.

If you want to play any type of wielder of arcane powers, chose Sorcery as your Priority A, B, or C, but choose carefully as the sorcerous Mysteries to which those Picks give your character access are all she will ever be able to learn; no sorcerer can ever learn more Mysteries than those allowed by his initial Sorcery Pick.

Once you have made your choice, create a background that fits your concept, discussing your choice with your fellow Players and the GM. This gives others the chance to riff off your inspiration and create character origins that will work well with your own.

A : Mysteriarch

A Mysteriarch is one of the greatest masters of the arcane alive. He can know all Mysteries, both Lesser and Greater, and he starts out the game with knowledge of a single Arcane Secret. He receives one free point in the Occultism Skill and one free Proficiency point to spend on Mysteries, but may not put more Proficiency points into Greater than into Lesser Mysteries.

B : Sorcerer

Sorcerer denotes somebody deeply steeped and well versed in arcane knowledge. She can know either all six Lesser Mysteries, or three Lesser and one Greater Mystery. She receives one free point in the Occultism Skill.

Should she opt for a Greater Mystery, it may be of no greater value than his highest Lesser Mystery.

C : Dabbler

Dabbler encompasses hedge wizards, witch doctors, sages of secret lore, some of the more devoted cultists of unwholesome deities and other serious meddlers in the occult. Such characters may know up to two Lesser Mysteries and may also have the Occultism Skill. Enslavement and Witchfire may not both be chosen.

D : Mundane

Mundane denotes the common man. He has heard whispers of sorcery but has most likely never encountered it, has no affinity for it, and most certainly doesn’t know any.

E : Cursed

Cursed denotes a person not only unskilled in the arcane, but also somehow particularly vulnerable to harmful sorcery. When she checks to resist sorcerous harm or influence, she does so at a +2 Target Number penalty.

F : Doomed

Doomed is a person not only devastatingly open to harmful sorcery, but actually victimized by it. He resists sorcery as per the E Pick, Cursed (at a +2 Target Number penalty), and on top of this has already contracted some kind of terrible supernatural doom. This doom is geared toward a specific, utterly ruinous fate, expressing itself in mechanical terms as the equivalent of a Curse Rating six (See the Cursing Mystery), which can be lifted by neither faith or sorcery.

This doom need not be geared toward the Character's death, but if it isn’t it needs to be geared to the total destruction of all the Character holds dear and wants to achieve. Whatever the doom may be, it must run its course, undoing the Character in some nasty fashion. Alternatively, it might be remotely possible to lift by means of an impossible quest. Such a quest should take up more than five game sessions devoted solely to it, during which time the Character (and likely his companions) should face more than even odds of dying at least three times; in short, the Game Master should make sure that such a quest fails more often than it succeeds.

Please be advised that this Priority Pick should not be taken lightly! The severity of the curse will more likely than not result in the destruction of the PC before the quest to lift it can be completed.

Culture

The Protagonists of the Sword & Sorcery genre are often quite a bit darker than those of heroic fantasy works like Tolkien’s Lord of the Rings. They tend to be outsiders or social outcasts, rejected or looked down upon by the powers of the society they find themselves in. Sword and Sorcery also diverges sharply from high fantasy in that non-humans such as elves and dwarves typically don’t exist, and both Player Characters and Non-Player Characters tend to be Human.

The archetypes listed are not meant as character classes, nor do they represent Human realities, but rather are descriptive clichés of the genre; tropes that can be used to distinguish characters that otherwise would have the same racial abilities.

A : Enlightened

A few great civilizations rise above others and gain near mythical status. Learned beyond normal men, people of enlightened cultures are builders of cyclopean pyramids and towers that pierce the skies. Their magnificent buildings can last forever, and likewise monuments to an enlightened one’s achievement can withstand the passage of time. Enlightened characters are especially long lived and begin play with these modifiers:

  • +1 each to Tenacity, Sagacity and Heart; minimum 2, maximum 8/9
  • One bonus supernatural or culture related Asset Occultism Skill always allowed
  • Fourteen (14) bonus Skill points
  • Enlightened Ones live half again to three times as long as mere mortals and need little to no sustenance or sleep

B : Savage

Savages include warriors from the frozen north and witch-doctors from the trackless wastes of the south. Savages tend to have a close connection with nature, but remain ignorant of many developments that more civilized people take for granted. Savage characters begin play with these modifiers:

  • +1 each to Brawn, Daring and Tenacity; minimum 2, maximum 8/9
  • Seven (7) bonus Skill points related to Survival, Climbing, Animal Ken, etc.

C : Hillman / Nomad

Hillmen live in broken mountainous regions that are hard to traverse. Their homeland lies either at the edge of civilized lands or is surrounded by them and might even be at least nominally part of a civilized nation. In any case, the isolation offered by their hills make Hillmen independent, and it is not uncommon for them to sally forth from their mountain fastness to prey upon softer people, forcibly collecting tribute from all who trespass their land.

Nomads roam the empty wastes beyond the civilized cities; quick to strike and bound by no laws. Nomads live with their animals and usually ride into battle with them; they feel ill at ease without them. Nomadic and Hillmen characters begin play with the following modifiers:

  • Seven (7) bonus Skill points related to Survival, Climbing, Animal Ken, etc.

D : Civilized

Savages and Hillmen eventually gather together to cultivate the land, build great cities, develop trade, and study medicine, mathematics and languages. In the civilized lands dwell noble knights, wise kings, and learned sages — as well as greedy merchants and cunning thieves. Civilized characters begin play with no particular advantages or disadvantages.

E : Decadent

Great civilizations reach their peak and eventually start to decline. Such fallen empires are ruled by jaded nobles, corrupt priests and wicked slave-traders. Demon-worship, human sacrifice and drug abuse is all too common in these cultures. Decadent characters begin play with the following modifiers:

  • -1 to Tenacity; minimum 1, maximum 7
  • Either -1 to Brawn (minimum 1, maximum 7) or one Poor Asset related to mental handicaps like Addiction, Sadism, etc.

F : Degenerate

The last survivors of decadent civilizations start to feud over dwindling resources, or are driven away by stronger cultures. Fleeing into the wilderness, or deep underground beneath their ruins, they start to inbreed and devolve into something no longer entirely human. Degenerates may outwardly resemble savages, but they carry the evil taint of fallen empires. Degenerate characters begin play with the following modifiers:

  • -1 to three Attributes; minimum 1, maximum 7
  • Two Poor Assets chosen by player, focused on bodily handicaps and gruesome mental illnesses such as Necrophilia, Inhuman, etc.

Attributes

Assigning points to your attributes is vital to constructing an exciting character. Each player is given a number of points to distribute amongst the six Basic Attributes. Available points are determined by the Priority level selected.

These points are assigned as you will, with but two cautions: All players must nominate one of their attributes as their “Focus.” No other attribute may exceed the point value of the “Focus” during the process of character creation.

In addition, no character should start with any Attribute at the unique level of nine. This level is reserved for development during play.

Next, work with your fellow players and Game Master to create your four Passion Attributes and fill them in on your character sheet. Beginning Passion Attribute Points are the same for each beginning character: Seven (7) points to be distributed between the four open Attributes and the fixed Drama Attribute. Any given Passion Attribute may be as low as zero or as high as five. Passion Attributes are described here.

Combined Attributes are calculated by combining two or more existing attributes. These should be calculated once all Basic and Passion Attributes are finalized. All numbers equal to or greater than .50 are rounded up.

  • Reflex = (Cunning + Daring) / 2
  • Aim = (Cunning + Sagacity) / 2
  • Knockdown = (Brawn + Daring) / 2
  • Knockout = (Brawn + Tenacity) / 2
  • Move = (Brawn + Cunning + Daring) / 3

Skills

Next, assign all available Skill points to those Skills you desire for your character. Again, available points are determined by the Priority level selected, and no character should begin initial play with any Skill at level nine. Pay close attention for any Sorcery, Cultural, or Asset modifiers to your Skills that you may be entitled to, and once each Skill has been chosen; write those Skills down on your sheet.

Proficiencies

Proficiencies refer to weapons' training. Each selected Melee Proficiency or Missile Proficiency conveys training in a single style of combat or grouping of weapons. As always, available points are determined by the Priority level selected. Points may be distributed in any manner the player desires, though no Proficiency may start out with the maximum rating of thirteen.

Once you have your Proficiencies assigned, write down any associated Maneuvers or Mysteries (Both are described in Chapters Three and Six respectively) on your character sheet.

Points granted by the Proficiency Priority can also be spent on sorcerous Mysteries, provided the character has access to any. Again, points may be distributed in any way the player desires.

Assets

Assets are aspects of a character’s personality, background, or physique, that make him better at some activities (Good) and worse at others (Poor) depending on which portion of the Asset is being explored. In addition to their mechanical effects, Assets suggest characteristics about the Character’s personality that might lead to interesting role-playing opportunities.

Assets offer a way for game mechanics to encourage deeper character backgrounds and more consistent role-playing.

Any character can begin play with up to six Good or three Poor Assets, chosen by the Player at the time of character creation, plus any other Assets the character may derive from other Picks such as Culture. On top of the Good or Poor Assets gained from his Picks, a player may opt to choose one (and only one), additional Good Asset for his character, if he balances this with a Poor Asset.

When selecting your “Good” or “Poor” Asset keep in mind that the entire Asset (both Good and Poor) is now yours to develop as you wish, but whatever form (again, Good or Poor) you initially selected it in will be the aspect that you first focus your role-playing efforts on.

Example: Picture a Barbarian who arrives in his first big city, a character that has begun the game with the Poor aspect of the “Uncivilized” Asset. He’s a savage man in civilized surroundings and therefore his asset would initially be rather detrimental to him.

But over time as he gradually gets accustomed to city life, he learns to better deal with his own uncivilized nature - he then begins to develop the Good aspect of the Asset, which reflects how he has learned to use his own savage nature to his advantage in certain situations!

With the passing of yet more adventure time, while he is still every bit as uncivilized as he was in the beginning (years before in some cases), he no longer suffers for it, and has apparently learned to compensate for it in other ways. The “Uncivilized” portion of his nature is truly now an advantage for him!


As soon as you feel that you have explored the current aspect of the Asset to your (and the group’s) satisfaction and would like to expand your role-playing vision, please feel free to purchase the Asset’s opposite side. You can then move between the two aspects of the Asset as desired. When no longer interested in the Asset you can always buy it off.

For a complete listing and description of Assets, click here.

Note that Poor Assets normally derived from the Culture Priority Pick can only be bought off under exceptional circumstances, and only then if both the referee and the rest of the gaming group agree that Characters experiences warrant such a radical development.
