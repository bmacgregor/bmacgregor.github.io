Passion Attributes

All Characters possess four Passion Attributes, or for short, Passions, each having a maximum value of five points. In addition, each Character has a special fixed Passion Attribute known as Drama.

Quite simply, Passion Attributes give your Character the will, drive, and determination to succeed when the going gets tough.

How They Work

If your Character is involved in a situation where he is struggling to defend, protect, further, or foster a Passion Attribute, that Passion gives its numerical value as extra dice added to the roll. For Attribute or Skill Checks, when one or more Passion potentially apply, the highest applicable Passion Attribute's value will be added to the appropriate dice pool for the Check.

For example, if you decide that one of your Passions is to Find all six artifacts of the first Emperor, and you find an ancient rune clue in a tomb you believe has one of the artifacts, then the Game Master might let you add your applicable Passion Attribute's equivalent dice value to your roll to read the rune.

In Combat, Passion Attributes work in a similar manner. However, instead the highest applicable Passion adding its value to every roll, while in combat, apply the highest applicable Passion Attribute, plus an extra die for every other applicable Passion Attribute, regardless of its current rating, as a bonus to the character’s Melee, Archery, and Sorcery Pools. All Passion Attributes should be collectively determined before play begins, with thought given to the harmony of the varied goals of all players participating in the session. Remember, Passion Attributes let you, the Players, collectively tell the Game Master exactly what you want the game to be about.

I Have To Make Them up Myself?

Yes, you do. This is understandably daunting, yet the most exciting task of Character Creation. Regardless of whatever else is written on that sheet, no matter how strong or weak the Characters' other Attributes, combat skills, magical aptitude, or skills are, nothing is more influential to that Character than those four lines.

While your four Passion Attributes define the general story being told, the Drama Passion fine tunes the result. Do you want to develop a love interest with the noblewoman you just met? Spend a Drama point! Do you need a convenient hay wagon to land in when leaping from a window? Spend a Drama point! Drama points can be used in most situations and are earned by roleplaying your character well.

Passions also advance my Character?

Yes, they do. Any time you role play and act on one of your Passion Attributes, the Game Master may reward your role playing with a Passion Attribute point. By acting on a Passion, it shows that it means something to your Character. As an aside, the numerical value of any Passion Attribute does not represent its importance to your Character or its priority over your other Passion Attributes; that's determined by your role playing alone, and merely defines which Passion is currently the focus of you, the Player.

So, by playing your Passion Attribute’s, they increase. Now, does the Game Master keep a tally of what you did during a session, and then dole points out at the end?

No! He immediately awards you with a point. The game is a dynamic field, where emotions and drama fluctuate naturally, instead of waiting for the pause between ‘episodes.’

Wait - If They get Awarded at Any Time...

That’s right! You can spend them at any time. Suddenly do you wish you were a bit stronger, or maybe a bit more nimble of mind? Then spend the points mid game, and it is so. See Chapter Two - Characters, and Chapter Three - Training for more information on spending Passion points.

Creating Passion Attributes

The scope of a Passion Attribute can be defined as either broad, narrow, or intermediate.

Assuming a player has the vague notion that he would like to have a series of adventures about his Character's attempts to become ruler of a nation, a Passion Attribute of Become King by My Own Hand could be considered a Passion Attribute with a broad scope. The same player, envisioning that his Character’s first intermediate step on his rise to the throne will be gaining political influence as protegé of the King’s current Grand Vizier might instead formulate a Passion Attribute of Win the Trust of the Grand Vizier. This could be considered a Passion Attribute of narrow scope. An intermediate position would be if the Player determines that his Character's rise to the throne is not to be by force, but by political means, which he leaves as of yet undefined, formulating a Passion Attribute of Amass Political Influence — an intermediate Passion Attribute.

Each of these three scopes have their own advantages and disadvanatges.

Broad Passion Attributes

The advantage of a broad Passion Attribute like Become King by My Own Hand is that the Passion Attribute can conceivably be considered to be engaged by many different scenarios and situations. Any activity that can be construed as bringing the Character closer to her distant goal, or even just in a position to better pursue this goal, can be considered to activate the Passion Attribute. This means that the Player will often have the mechanical benfit of the Passion Attribute. It also makes it easy for the GM to present the Player with situations and scenes where the Passion will be engaged.

The disadvantage of the broad Passion lies in its fuzziness. If you are playing a Character who still has far to go to assume the mantle of kingship, a broad Passion Attribute like Become King by My Own Hand doesn't really tell the Game Master much about what adventures the Player would like to have right now. A Character who’s a penniless, unconnected vagabond with such a Passion Attribute can hardly be presented with an opening to become King right away. While a broad Passion will often be activated, it tells the GM little about what the Player would like to have adventures about, and the Player will thus find himself frequently at the fringes of scenarios that are tailored more closely to Players who communicated their wishes in a narrow frame and thus, will appear in a supporting role.

Narrow Passion Attributes

The advantage of a narrow Passion Attribute like Win the Trust of the Grand Vizier is that it tells the Game Master exactly what the player wants to have adventures about. She wants to have adventures where she can make herself useful to the Grand Vizier and rise in his esteem. She tells this to the GM clearly, and the GM knows what kind of elements to build into the dynamic situation she prepares for the scenario to engage this Player and her narrow Passion Attribute.

The disadavantage of the narrow Passion Attribute is that, while it's clear in defining the Player’s expectations, it will only be engaged by select and specific circumstances. A situation that offers - for example - an opportunity to gain favour with the King himself would not engage the Passion Attribute orientated towards the Grand Vizier. Unless a narrow Passion Attribute is changed frequently — about every second or third session — it will lead to extremely repetitive adventures.

There is a second, potential disadvantage of narrow Passion Attributes, one that enters play in groups with many Players and becomes aggravated if narrow Passion Attributse abound amongst them. It can be difficult, sometimes substantially so, for a Game Master to come up with situations that engage many narrow Passions. If everyone has a narrow interest, finding something that piques everbody’s interest isn’t easy.

Intermediate Passion Attributes

Intermediate Passion Attributes like Amass Political Influence combine the advantages and disadvanatges from both broad and narrow Passion Attributes to a lesser extent. It tells the Game Master more than the broad Passion Attribute, without limiting him in the way the narrow one does. It will be engaged frequently, without the resulting adventures appearing as copies of one another.

While it might appear that an intermediate scope is "best", it shouldn't be thought of that way. Broad and narrow Passions can be just as fun and just as useful, if they are mixed well. A Player Character with only broad Passions will be just as problematic as a Character with only narrow Passions. The secret’s in a good mixture: A broad Passion Attribute or two to cut the GM some slack in coming up with scenarios and situations engaging your Character and to provide yourself with some broadly applicable bonus dice, and a narrow Passion or two to exactly tell the Game Master what you want out of the game right now and to afford him an opportunity to really make your Character into an episode’s main protagonist.

Wide and Tight Passion Attribute Spreads

A second item to consider in creating a Character’s Passion Attributes is their "spread", that is to say, whether they're geared toward the pursuit of several different goals or pointed at closely related ones. For the sake of this discussion, I am going to call the former a "wide" spread, and the latter a "tight" spread.

The Wide Spread

A wide spread - one where a Character’s Passions have little to no overlap - has the advantage of being easy on the Game Master. As the player communicates a wide range of interests to pursue through scenarios, the GM should have little difficulty in involving that character in a scenario. The advatage to the Player is that she is virtually assured that one of her Passion Attributes will be engaged in every scene of every scenario. If it isn’t, the GM really isn’t doing her job, considering how easy it was made for hrt by the Player in setting up a wide spread of Passion Attributes.

The didsadvantage of such a wide spread is that the Character will rarely encounter situations where more than one of his Passion Attributes are engaged, meaning less bonus dice, both in combat and sorcery, where the highest engaged Passion Attribute adds its rating in dice, plus one die for every other engaged Passion Attribute, as everywhere else, where only the highest engaged Passion Attribute adds its rating — if only one Passion Attributes happens to be activated, there is no choice of applying the “highest” activated one.

The Tight Spread

A tight spread, where most or all of a Character’s Passion Attributes do overlap, has the advantage that several of them will usually be engaged at once and thus of providing more bonus dice.

The disadvantage is mainly one for the GM, and it is similar to the one of narrow Passion Attributes: If a Player expresses interest in only one pursuit, it can be hard for the Game Master to consistently come up with scenes that pique this interest and engage the Passion Attributes, especially if the group is large. If a Player is as unkind to his GM as to give him only a tight spread of Passion Attributes to work with in designing scenes, the Game Master should be excused if he does occasionally frame scenes that do not engage any of the Passions of that Player. This is doubly true if the group is large, and triply so if that Player’s Passions are also mostly narrow in scope (though it will be rare to have a tight spread of narrow Passion Attributes; a mixture of broad, narrow and intermediate Passion Attributes clustered tightly will be more common).
