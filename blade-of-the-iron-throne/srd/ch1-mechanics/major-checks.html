Dice Types Used

Most of the time in gaming, you don’t use a rule; you make a ruling. The Players can describe any action without needing to look at a Character sheet to see if they can do it. The Game Master, in turn, uses common sense to decide what happens or rolls a die only if she thinks there’s some random element involved. When needed, Blade uses two types of dice during play, twelve-sided dice (d12) and six-sided dice (d6). References throughout this book refer to twelve-sided dice unless otherwise noted.

Major Checks

In Blade, Checks are made by rolling an appropriate number of dice for the Attribute or Skill level being tested against a fixed Target Number (or TN) of seven. The number of successes needed for both Attribute and Skill Checks are drawn from the following table.

Successes Needed

Successes Challenge Level *
One Average
Two Challenging
Three Difficult
Four Amazing
Five Legendary

* If the applicable dice pool is more than three times the required number of successes, no roll is needed, instead the Character is considered to have achieved the amount of successes.

Only dice rolled that meet or exceed the Target Number are successful. With the exception of Proficiency and Mystery Checks, all Checks are made against a Target Number of seven, unless modified by Assets. Quality of Success may also denote an action’s degree of success as indicated in the following table.

Quality of Success

Net Number of Successes Result
Zero Failure
(Tie) Result Varies
One Basic Success
Two Good
Three Well Done
Four Expertly Done
Five Perfect

While only one success is normally needed for an action to be successful, some actions at the GM’s discretion may be tasked with additional success requirements. One example could be a Player Character attempting to pick a lock while pressed for time:

GM: You want to pick this lock? That would normally be only Average, but to do it in time is Challenging. You’re going to need two successes. Getting no successes will mean things get complicated! (See the section on Complications later in this chapter)

Player: Damn! I don’t have Lock Picking as a skill! Can I try it using that lock-pick Otho gave me?

GM: Sure - but it’ll be tougher since you don’t know what you’re doing. Now you’re going to need four successes. And any result of two or less successes will earn you a Complication.

In this example one success would normally achieve the task (opening the lock), but an additional success is required to achieve the intended result (opening it before something else happens), for a total of two successes.


Attempting a task as an Attribute Check instead of a Skill Checkdoubles the number of successes needed. Blade follows the idea that one action requires one Check. There are three basic types of Checks:

Attribute Checks

Attribute Checks are made with a die pool derived from an Attribute level. They represent tasks attempted with one’s natural, inborn ability - one’s talent - with abilities one has picked up along the way without formal training.

Skill Checks

Skill Checks are tests made with a die pool derived from a Skill level. Skills reflect a Character’s aptitude in a trained, acquired pursuit. The degree of capability a Character can attain through training is limited by his talent for it. Wwhen using a Skill, a Character’s Skill dice pool may never equal or exceed twice the value of the Attribute that would be most applicable for the task attempted.

For example, a Character with a Cunning of three means that he can use no more than five dice from the Lock Picking Skill in any situation.

Conversely, Characters unskilled in a particular discipline work at a disadvantage compared to their skilled counterparts. They may attempt Checks by using the its corresponding Attribute, but require twice as many successes as someone who knows the Skill.

Example: Otho wants to accomplish a task requiring the Animal Ken Skill, but is untrained in it. He has a Heart of six, and therefore may use it for the Check instead. If someone skilled in Animal Ken would normally need two successes to succeed at a specific Animal Ken Check, Otho would instead need four.


Skill levels are generally rated as per the following table.

Skill Levels

Zero Default for untrained characters
One Beginner; Most students
Two Apprentice; Growing in skill
Three - Four Journeyman; Trained professionals
Five - Seven Adept; Skilled and respected veterans
Eight - Nine Master; Well-known and sought after

Attribute and Skill values range from one to eight, with one Skill and Attribute each at a maximum level of nine per Player or NPC. No two Characters may have a score of nine in the same Attribute or Skill. NPCs are not bound by this stricture and may have the same Skill or Attribute at level nine that his Player counterpart enjoys, however, for each Attribute or Skill, there may only be a single NPC per campaign who has this Attribute or Skill at level nine. If this NPC dies or otherwise leaves the campaign, he may not be replaced by another NPC with a score of nine in that Attribute or Skill for the rest of the campaign.

Skill Check Challenge Levels should always frame the task in terms of how hard it is for someone who does it all the time. They are invariably rolled against a Target Number seven (TN7), unless modified by an Asset.

Example: Having gained admission to the Radja’s dinner ball, Otho prowls the halls in search for his intended pickpocket victim. The GM advises that the society vultures are unusually acerbic tonight and that Otho will have to bluff his way through several social encounters before locating the victim. He declares drawing no attention whatsoever to him a Challenging (two successes needed) feat of Etiquette.

Proficiency Checks

Proficiency Checks are made with a dice pool calculated from several contributing scores, and are done by rolling any number of dice drawn from the appropriate pool (either the Melee, Archery, or Sorcery Pools, respectively) against Target Numbers that frequently differ from the usual Target Number of seven. They are more fully explained in chapters four (Combat) and six (Sorcery).

Opposed Checks

Opposed Checks are made whenever two Characters or forces match each other for supremacy. Examples include attacking an opponent, detecting an ambush, or slipping past a patrol.

All manner of Checks may be tested against any other, with one Player possibly attempting a Skill Check, while her opponent rolls an Attribute Check in opposition. With Opposed Checks, both participants roll against Target Number seven. The number of successes of the participant with the smaller number of successes are subtracted from the other participant’s, leaving the latter as the winner with a Quality of Success as an indicator of the general degree of victory.

For example, if Otho is trying to lie to a Street Thug, both roll their Falsehood skill against a Target Number of seven. Otho rolls a 12, 8, 7, and 4 for a total of three successes, while the Thug rolls a 10, 3, and a 2, achieving one success. The Thug's successes are then subtratced from Otho's (3 - 1) leaving Otho with a final result - or Quality of Success - of two.

If an Opposed Skill Check is called for and one party lacks the required Skill, he may substitute the most appropriate Attribute as normal, but must compare his result to twice the number of successes of his opponent.

Ties are counted as a success for the party who wants to maintain the status quo. In picking somebody’s pockets, a tie would result in a failure for the thief. In attempting to avoid detection by a guard, a tie would keep the thief hidden.

A Quality of Success of one will always achieve the desired task. If the Game Master deems this too easy (thinking that additional factors would make the task harder to achieve for one than the other party) he is advised not to call for an Opposed Check, but for a straight-forward (but difficult) Attribute or Skill Check instead.

If an Opposed Check matches one Character against several others at the same time, a single roll is made for every Character involved. The solitary character compares his result to each opponent’s roll. For example, if a thief tries to sneak past two soldiers standing guard, one roll is made for the thief and one for every soldier. The thief’s result is then compared separately to both results, for two Qualities of Success scores.

Example: Otho makes his move at the ball and invites a rich old dowager (and her beautiful diamond necklace!) to dance. As they swirl about the dance floor he directs her to a somewhat unobserved spot and tries to remove the necklace. An Opposed Check with two antagonists is now called for: The guard closest to the pair makes a Check using his Sagacity (4) Attribute, testing his powers of observation, and the dowager checks using her Sagacity (3) Attribute, to determine if she is aware of the necklace being removed.

Otho makes a single Check using Pick Pockets (6) against both opponents. Otho needs to tie the guard’s result to pocket the necklace unobserved by the guard (the status quo being that the guard notices nothing special), but he needs to trump the dowager’s successes to filch the necklace (the status quo being that she has it).

Unluckily, Otho achieves a single successes, the guard rolls one success, and the dowager rolls a somewhat lucky two successes. While the guard would not have noticed anything out of the ordinary, the dowager feels Otho’s groping fingers and raises a ruckus, leaving Otho to bluff his way out of a tight spot.
